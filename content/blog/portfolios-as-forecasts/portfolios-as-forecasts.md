## Expressed vs. revealed preference

Let me spoil the fundamental thesis of this post right at the start: almost everyone now has stated probabilities about AI outcomes, and almost no one's allocation agrees with their mouth. We all know someone who professes daily that wages are cooked and labour trends to zero, yet whose entire net worth rests, hilariously, on a promotion cycle. This isn't a new finding - in fact, it might be one of the few reliably replicable findings in economics. Hypothetical bias: people systematically overstate what they'd pay when the statement costs nothing. Or, in other words, _talk is cheap_. And it really is! This blog costs me nothing.

What is expensive, however, is _choice_. We measure the cost of choice in _opportunity cost_, with some caveats. At the level of the individual, this can be almost impossible to quantify. We've all heard it before, _What's better, 80 percent chance at $1000, or 1 percent chance at a million?_ Well, it depends. Can you make rent this month?

In this way and a million others, individual utility functions are extremely difficult to measure. How do we price in the cost of precarity? How do we price in loss aversion bias? People are complex in ways that are difficult to model, and some of the best examples are even motivated by utility functions other than profit. You can see where i'm going with this.

## The tines of causality

But positions on what, exactly? Before we read the book, we need the contracts. I've seen a lot of terms bandied around (and maybe you have too!) - Permanent underclass (the perennial favourite), fast takeoff, slow takeoff, singularity. I want to try and distill the intellectual brinkmanship into what i think are three meaningful, more or less agree upon buckets of likely outcome, based on a rigorous factor analysis of _the vibes I've witnessed_.

**The fizzle.** Capabilities plateau somewhere near current-plus-incremental. AI becomes extremely good middleware - maybe an excellent spreadsheet, maybe iron man Jarvis - but never generalises into replacing judgment wholesale. Labour gets reshuffled, not devalued. In twenty years this era looks either a lot like the dot com bubble, or more likely, the diffusion of the power loom: real technology, hysterical narratives, but survivable distributional outcome. There is churn - industrial-revolution-grade churn - but spread across a generation rather than a presidential term. Interestingly, the ceiling of this path gets ratcheted up with every new model release (one more OOM bro, i promise bro, one more order of magnitude and it generalises). This particular fizzle means the diffusion of fable 5 tier models, which is where the frontier is as of writing.

This is our likely floor at t+1.

**The compression**. The fizzle without the endpoint. Capabilities keep improving, and the boundary between replaceable and irreplaceable work becomes a waterline that keeps rising. Generic cognitive labour deflates the way manufacturing labour did, steadily, with everyone insisting their floor is different. This is (arguably) the fat middle of the distribution, and it splits along two broad axes that matter for our purposes:
1. Ownership: does frontier capability commoditise (everyone gets cheap leverage) or concentrate (a few firms own the rails and rent cognition back to you)? 
2. Speed: a thirty-year compression is the industrial revolution again, with the generational mercy; an eight-year compression is functionally a discontinuity for anyone mid-career. Same tine, radically different worlds. 
The permanent underclass - the perennial favourite - lives in exactly one cell: _fast, concentrated, no institutional response_. It's a real possible outcome - but not the whole tine.

**The discontinuity.** Capabilities don't level off. The world on the other side isn't legible from here, in either direction — the abundance branch dissolves the labour/capital frame entirely, and the bad branch makes your asset allocation not the binding constraint on your wellbeing. Notably, almost no personal strategy is robust to this tine, which is why almost nobody allocates for it. Hold that thought; it'll matter when we read the institutional book.

Tines is a clumsy metaphor here. Outcomes are continuous, and it's hard to say how they might branch from here. But for our purposes, these are the buckets we will use (call it an engineer's estimate).

## Reading the T(bill) leaves

So if individuals are unreadable (precarity, rent, loss aversion, utility functions that don't reduce to profit) who _can_ we read? Institutions. Institutions are the cleanest subjects revealed preference ever gets. Their constraints are disclosed in filings. Their allocation decisions are made deliberately, repeatedly, by committees whose entire job is to bet the balance sheet on a view of the future. They are incentive-compatible at billion-dollar scale, and - unlike your friend with the promotion cycle - an institution cannot claim it was just making rent. (It can, actually. Its rent is quarterly. We'll get to that.)

Which means we can do something fun: treat the corporate landscape as a prediction market. Not the keynotes (well known to be the mouth). The capex, the acquisitions, the charters applied for, the balance sheet composition. Every one of these is a position on the tines, sized in billions and marked to market. Polymarket for the end of wages, except the bettors are forced to be honest because the money is already down - and if there's one institution set i feel qualified to opine on, it's the moneyers.

So let's read the book

**The charter stampede.** Eleven companies filed for or received OCC national trust bank charters in eighty-three days — Circle, Ripple, Paxos, Fidelity, Bridge, Morgan Stanley among them, with Coinbase pending behind. No coordinated announcement. Eleven independent actors, crypto-natives to century-old banks, reading the same odds and placing the same bet inside one quarter — that's a market consensus caught mid-formation. And note what the bet is _not_ on: technology. A charter is an expensive, irreversible commitment to OCC supervision — capital requirements, examinations, a permanent leash. Eleven firms pricing the leash as worth wearing are eleven firms telling you the settlement tech is commoditized and _permission_ is the scarce asset.

Yes, the timing is supply-side — the GENIUS Act created the license, a friendly OCC opened the door, a queue formed. But supply explains timing, not willingness to pay. And the rush itself is information: filing in the first eighty-three days is a bet the window closes. The bank lobby reportedly weighing litigation to shut the door is its own revealed preference — you don't sue to stop people acquiring something worthless.

**The incumbents' counter.** JPMorgan, Citi, BofA, Wells Fargo and a dozen peers announced a shared tokenized deposit network via The Clearing House, targeting 2027 — explicitly to defend against deposit flight that Standard Chartered sized at up to $1 trillion by 2028. The entire position fits in one sentence: a tokenized deposit never leaves the bank's balance sheet. Same cryptography, opposite answer to who holds the money while it moves. This is permission _and_ distribution played from strength: we have the licenses, we have the depositors, we'll adopt your rails and keep our seat. That one of its own architects admitted clients aren't "beating down the door" tells you it's a hedge, not a conviction — but institutions pay rent too. Theirs is quarterly. (Told you we'd get to that.)

**The commons.** Meanwhile the open rails compounded without asking anyone: stablecoin supply doubled in two years to ~$320 billion, and settlement volume hit $33 trillion in 2025 — past Visa's annual throughput. Cross-chain movement went from the industry's recurring catastrophe to boring plumbing, which is what winning looks like for a commons. This is the commoditized cell's position: rails are a utility, value settles at the edges. Though even here, watch the allocation over the keynote — Circle, the commons' flagship, is building Arc, its own L1 with USDC as native gas, partners including BlackRock and Visa. Nobody picks a cell. They buy the spread.

**The absent position.** Everything above is some flavour of compression — the factions disagree only on which moat dominates, permission or distribution. Nobody in the moneyers' book is positioned for the fizzle (nobody gets funded to bet on nothing happening) and nobody can price the discontinuity — except one set of institutions adjacent to the book: hyperscalers committing roughly $725 billion in 2026 capex, ~64% up year over year, borrowing to do it because the spend now exceeds their cash generation. The only bettors at the table leveraging up. Adjust your read accordingly: the book gives you odds over compression cells, not over the whole tree.

The synthesis, and it's a homology, not a causal claim: the moneyers aren't forecasting the compression — they're _rehearsing_ it. Their execution layer commoditized a decade early, and their response is the playbook capital runs whenever the scarce thing stops being scarce: mark technical moats to zero, race for permission, buy distribution. AI is running the same commoditization on a vastly wider class of work. Read the book not as odds on AI, but as a preview of what every industry's book looks like when the waterline arrives.
